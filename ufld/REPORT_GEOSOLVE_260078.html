1Headline — the full pipeline: vision + geometry support

The lane is read from each image by the UFLD v3 lane detector (vision) — the core of the pipeline. The GPS + road-database geometry stage is its supporting signal: a nearly-free prior that cross-checks every vision reading and supplies the official (RAMM) lane count. When the two agree the frame is labelled automatically; otherwise it goes to human review. Frames with an empty manual label are skipped up front (1,335 of 1,322,163, 0.1%); the hard cases marked Ignore are processed like everything else.

Division of labour: vision does the actual reading of the road; geometry is cheap enough to run on every survey in seconds, so it always rides along as the cross-check. Fusion numbers above are measured on the 3,272-frame evaluation sample — the full-survey vision run is in progress (11 of 47 survey days completed as of 2026-07-12; timing figures above are measured on those days). Until it completes, full-survey production numbers come from the geometry stage alone (map-match 97.7%, auto 83.1% @ 95.4% — baseline A in §4).

About the ground truth (read before quoting accuracies).

20,585 frames are marked Ignore by the labelling team — these are the genuinely hard situations (vehicle parked on the roadside, or driving between two lanes). They are processed like every other frame; today the system routes most of them to review, and a future model version should learn to classify these situations explicitly. A further 15,904 frames carry special codes (SK/TK/4/5…) outside the 1/2/3 lane scheme — always routed to human review and excluded from the accuracy denominator.

The labels themselves are not perfect: spot checks found a small number of wrongly entered lane labels (one of this report's own example images turned out to be mislabelled — see §5), and labels are entered as time segments (laneFixes), so frames near a lane-change boundary can inherit the neighbouring lane's label. Where geometry and vision independently agree yet the label says otherwise (~1.9% of agreeing frames), the label itself is often the error. All accuracies below are therefore lower bounds.

2How the pipeline is put together

The vision model reads the lane from each image; geometry supports it with an independent, nearly-free second opinion. Because the two sources fail in different ways, their agreement is the strongest correctness signal we have measured.

Vision (UFLD v3) — primarylane-line detector on the image → lane strips → ego lane number, counted from the centerline side.

4Decision policies — coverage vs accuracy

A policy is the rule that decides which frames get auto-labelled and which go to human review — in essence, how much geometry confirmation a vision reading needs before the pipeline trusts it. We simulated six candidate rules on the labelled sample; each trades coverage (how much is automated) against accuracy (how often the automation is right). A is the geometry-only fallback used while the vision run completes.

Chart 3 · Each dot is one candidate decision rule

Horizontal: share of frames the rule auto-labels — everything not covered goes to human review. Vertical: how often those auto labels match the manual label. Up = more accurate, right = less review work. Blue dots = the two recommended rules.

How to choose: if accuracy matters most, pick B — auto-label only when geometry and vision independently agree: 98.1% right, 25.4% of frames left for review. The balanced recommendation is F — B plus vision where it passes the RAMM lane-count gate: 96.8% right, 20.9% to review. C or G push coverage to ~89–92% but drop to ~94% accuracy.

5What it looks like

6Key findings

  • Agreement is the money signal. When geometry and vision agree, the label is right 98.1% of the time (74.6% of frames) — vs 95.6% for the current geometry-only auto rule. Most of the remaining 1.9% "disagreements with GT" trace back to label errors, not system errors.
  • Vision owns multilane. On actual lane-2 frames vision is 84.4% vs geometry’s 10.3%. On 2-lanes/dir roads: 81.3% vs 34.4%.
  • Lane 3 stays with humans. Both sources are <6% accurate on lane 3 (n=94) — keep it flagged for review (and note the GT itself is noisiest there).
  • The ground truth has known noise. Spot checks found a small number of wrong lane labels, and the 20,585 Ignore frames mark genuinely ambiguous situations (parked / between lanes) — see the note in section 1. Reported accuracies are lower bounds.
  • Error magnitude is small. When either source is wrong it is almost always off by exactly one lane (off-by-2+ < 1%).
  • Turning / intersections: vision loses its lane view (coverage drops to 76%) but stays accurate when it does answer; geometry is unaffected (96% while turning).

7Next steps

  1. Ship policy F (agree → auto; disagree → auto-vision only if it passes the RAMM gate; else review): 96.8% accuracy at 79.1% coverage, or policy G for maximum coverage.
  2. Decode refinement in the lane detector: drop phantom strips (high-RMSE boundaries) before numbering, and anchor numbering on the detected centerline slot instead of the right-most strip.
  3. Temporal smoothing — measured (2026-07-11): dense inference on 9,113 consecutive frames (2 multilane drives of ~25 min + 23 windows around known error spots) confirms the flicker hypothesis: a 5-frame majority vote (valid only when same road, not turning, gaps < 1.5 s) lifts vision accuracy on multilane roads from 92.7% → 95.2% and halves flicker (9.4 → 4.3 lane switches per 100 frames; 230 frames fixed vs 35 broken). On 1-lane roads the vote changes nothing (errors there are systematic, not noise) — exactly as predicted. Bearing stays a negative gate only (don’t vote while turning). Source: output/geosolve_260078/eval_temporal_vote/, script scripts/eval_temporal_vote.py.
  4. Model v4 data: add 3+ lane motorway scenes and roadside-negative examples (berms, parking, cycle lanes) — the two measured failure modes. Also add the Ignore situations (vehicle parked on the roadside, driving between two lanes) as explicit classes, so the model can name these cases instead of forcing a lane number on them.
